Chromium

  • \(\ce{Cr}\) is an extremely lustrous metal. It is somewhat brittle, and somewhat resistant to corrosion
  • Pure \(\ce{Cr}\) is blue-white in color. Elemental \(\ce{Cr}\) is paramagnetic
  • Chromium naturally occurs in chromium ores. The major chromium ore is chromite (\(\ce{FeCr2O4}\)). There is also another ore of chromium called magnesiochromite (\(\ce{MgCr2O4}\))
  • Chromite may be oxidized to the soluble \(\ce{CrO4^2-}\) under basic conditions, and later be reduced to the amphoteric \(\ce{Cr2O3}\) with \(\ce{C}\), and further reduced to \(\ce{Cr}\) with \(\ce{Al}\)
  • The chromium metal gets oxidized by oxygen to \(\ce{Cr2O3}\) at room temperature
  • Chromium does not react with water, but dissolves in most acids
  • Chromium shows what is known as self-passivation. It reacts spontaneously with \(\ce{O2}\) and forms an oxide coating around it. This coating is very hard and unreactive. Therefore, \(\ce{Cr}\) is used to electroplate metals and harden the surfaces of tools
